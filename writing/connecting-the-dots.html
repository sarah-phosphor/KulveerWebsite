Connecting the Dots

In 2005 I read "eBoys" by Randall Stross while commuting to my job at Deutsche Bank, shortly after graduating from Oxford. The book chronicled venture capital operations at Benchmark Capital during the tech boom through embedded journalism, which I found compelling for capturing authentic moments while minimizing narrative bias.

Inspired by this approach, I asked a journalist friend about documenting mine and Harj's startup journey with Auctomatic — though I acknowledge this naive optimism about our story's interest level.

Six years later, Stross emailed announcing he would be embedded with Y Combinator during their summer program for his next book. I immediately reached out to express my excitement about the opportunity and Stross's previous work's impact.

The resulting book, "The Launch Pad," had just been published. Upon receiving it, I found myself immediately absorbed, particularly by coverage of partner office hours from the previous summer when my team and I launched Tagstand.

Reviewing past conversations proved surprising — I had limited recall of specific discussions, and startup dynamics had shifted so dramatically that some strategies now seemed embarrassing. However, I appreciated Stross's unvarnished approach, presenting firsthand accounts without "glossy make-overs."

I acknowledge the organizational risks of granting unprecedented access, but praise Paul Graham for permitting this transparency. The book documents YC's expansion phase, demonstrating the organization's willingness to experiment.

The work also provides historical context, including details about Harj and me applying in 2006 despite living overseas and being dismissed as "business guys."

Just as "eBoys" educated me at 22 with minimal Valley connections, "The Launch Pad" will serve aspiring founders globally.

What I believe

01

High output and customer obsession are the two necessary conditions.

Not sufficient for success. But necessary. The best YC companies I've ever seen — they all had both. Every single week there's forward progress. It doesn't always mean a new feature — it's the speed of learning.

02

Be the best in the world at something narrow.

"Reducto — when I remember that pitch, it was like, we're just trying to understand a PDF. Which felt narrow. But if you're actually legitimately the best in the world at that, that's fucking huge."

03

Burnout comes from people, not from work.

Working hard while making progress is energizing. The burnout I experienced at Zeus — it came from people. Brilliant hires who weren't aligned. Investors who wanted control. The biggest risk to building a decade-long company is losing your energy.

04

The outliers won't be obvious today.

The biggest companies of the next decade aren't going to look like obvious bets right now. I've been around YC enough to know that things like this work. It always looks a bit fragile in the early days.

05

It's honestly a little bit of vibes.

Sometimes I'm sitting there writing my investment memo and I'm like — what's a more sophisticated way of saying I think these founders are amazing? I've been to every Demo Day since 2007. If I know, I know.

"The modern day incarnation of Alexander the Great, Caesar, Napoleon — it's tech founders. It is in their mind, it's warfare. There's territory out there to be conquered. Either you conquer it or someone else will."

On customer obsession

The Stripe bug-fix

Patrick had this rule where every engineer was on call 24/7. Laptop on you at all times — watching a movie, on a date, asleep. Users would hit a bug on Stripe, start opening their laptops to write an email, and by the time they sent it, Stripe would ping them: "Hey, we noticed you had an issue. It's fixed now."

People were like, whoa, what the fuck?

On choosing investors

The investors I'd rather forget

I raised from Soros, Susquehanna — the people that own 20% of ByteDance. They don't give a shit about YC or Bookface reviews or anything. They're brutal. I had so much pressure to sell.

The truth is I sold my company for less than it was worth because I wanted to get these people out of my life. I never wanted to sell. I've taken a lot of my rebound energy into trying to help founders avoid what I went through.

On VC red flags

When the VC thinks it's their company

One VC — in the first meeting with an amazing company — says, okay, I need you to triple revenue in three months. Wants monthly pseudo board meetings. The founder told me, "It's really weird, but it feels like I report to the VC."

VCs that act like it's their company. Big red flag.
